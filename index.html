Welcome to Orchid Conservation

Orchids are one of nature’s most beautiful and endangered flowers. Join us in learning about these fascinating plants and the conservation efforts to protect them.

Our Orchid Conservation Journey

Late 1800s

The discovery of the Blue Vanda and White Nun Orchid sparked high demand and over-collection, putting them at risk.

1930s

Lycaste virginalis becomes the national flower of Guatemala, but it is already rare due to over-collection.

1940s

Guatemala bans the export of orchids to protect them from being over-collected.

1990s

Guatemala lifts the ban on orchid exports, but conservation efforts remain strong.

2024 (Present)

Research continues into the medicinal properties of the Blue Vanda, aiming for sustainable farming methods.

Present Day

The North American Orchid Conservation Center (NAOCC) is actively working to conserve native orchids. Exhibitions are raising awareness of orchid conservation efforts.

2024

The Smithsonian Gardens and U.S. Botanic Garden host "The Future of Orchids: Conservation and Collaboration" exhibition. This exhibition raises awareness about orchid conservation and showcases 3D scans of orchids, highlighting the potential of 3D digitization for research, education, and conservation efforts.

Orchid 1: Vanda coerulea

This orchid is called the Blue Vanda because of its large, blue flowers, which amazed people when it was first brought to Europe in 1847. Because of its beauty, many expeditions were made to collect the orchid, putting it in danger. Today, scientists are studying the orchid’s medicinal properties. In India, the flowers have been used to help treat chest problems, and the leaf juice has been used for treating diarrhea, dysentery, and skin issues. The Blue Vanda has an upright flower stem that can grow up to 2 feet long and can have several long-lasting white flowers with a blue crosshatch pattern. Each flower is about 4 inches wide and has a lovely fragrance. It blooms from August to December in the Northern Hemisphere, with the peak blooming season in November. Sadly, the Blue Vanda is still at risk due to habitat loss and the effects of over-collection in the past. Protecting this orchid and its natural environment is vital to keep it thriving for future generations to appreciate.

Orchid 2: Psychopsis versteegiana

This orchid, named after a Dutch plant collector, is known for its bright and beautiful flowers. Its tall stem can grow 26–44 inches long and usually has 3–5 orange and yellow flowers that bloom one at a time. Each flower is about 3 inches wide and looks like a butterfly, which is why it’s called a "Butterfly Orchid." These orchids grow on trees in tropical rainforests in South America. They don’t harm the trees—they just use them for support. Sadly, because of habitat loss and over-collection, these amazing orchids are at risk. Protecting them and their natural habitats is important to ensure that future generations can continue to enjoy their beauty. Their colorful, butterfly-like flowers make them popular with people who love orchids, and efforts to conserve them help keep these unique plants alive.

Orchid 3: Phragmipedium × richteri

The Phragmipedium × richteri 'Wolf River' is a type of orchid known for its unique, pouch-like flowers. This orchid grows on the ground and is part of the Orchidaceae family, which includes many beautiful and interesting plants. The 'Wolf River' variety is cultivated, meaning it’s grown in controlled conditions and not found in the wild. It’s currently displayed at the Smithsonian Gardens' National Portrait Gallery. Even though it’s not wild, it's important to protect all types of orchids and their habitats because many species face threats from habitat loss and over-collection. Conservation efforts help keep these amazing plants safe for the future.
